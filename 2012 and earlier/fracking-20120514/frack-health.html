Nitrogen Oxides

Are They Contributing To Lung Problems?

Compressors pressurize and push gas through pipelines. In some places they're located inside buildings, which cuts down the noise but doesn't necessarily cut down on pollution.

The engines that power compressors and the trucks and heavy equipment used in gas production all release gases known as nitrogen oxides. These gases can irritate the lungs of healthy people and worsen breathing problems in people with asthma.

Nitrogen oxides can also react with other things in the air to form small particles that are dangerous for people with bronchitis, emphysema and heart disease.

Studies show that emergency room visits, hospital admissions, heart attacks and even deaths are more common when there are high levels of nitrogen oxides in the air.

Ozone

Is There Enough To Harm Vulnerable Lungs?

We depend on ozone high in the atmosphere to help protect us from the sun's rays. But when lots of ozone forms near the ground, it can affect people's health -- especially people who already have asthma or other lung problems.

Studies show that in some areas, gas wells are a major contributor to ozone. That's because gas operations release both nitrogen oxides and chemicals known as volatile organic compounds (VOCs), which react in the presence of sunlight to create ozone.

At least one study found that in the Dallas-Fort Worth area -- home to nearly 16,000 gas wells -- natural gas production was more of a problem than motor vehicles when it came to emissions that form ozone.

Living In Gas Country

Health And Fracking: A Big Question Without Good Answers

There's no question that the tens of thousands of fracked gas wells in states like Colorado, Texas and Pennsylvania are putting potentially dangerous chemicals into the environment.

But scientists can't tell whether these chemicals are making people sick without answering some difficult questions for each chemical:

  • How much have people been exposed to?
  • How long has the exposure been going on?
  • How much of the exposure is the result of gas extraction?
  • Do exposed people have health problems known to be caused by a given chemical?
  • Did these health problems exist before the exposure began?
  • How frequent are these health problems in people who were not exposed?

Getting answers to those questions will take millions of dollars and years of work.

Methane

Safe To Breathe, But A Factor In Global Warming

Methane is the main component of natural gas, and companies want to sell all they can. But before they can get the gas flow from a well into a pipeline, some companies vent methane gas directly into the air. Methane also leaks from pipes, valves, compressors and tanks involved in gas production and distribution.

Breathing small amounts of methane does not present a health hazard, according to the EPA. But people can suffocate if natural gas displaces oxygen in an enclosed space. And, of course, methane can burn or explode.

Scientists say the biggest problem with methane is that it is a potent greenhouse gas and a significant contributor to global warming.

Volatile Organic Compounds

Hazardous Air Pollutants: How Much?

Wells produce gas for years -- hopefully decades. And they can continue to release toxic chemicals long after they're hooked up to a pipeline: volatile organic compounds such as benzene, ethylbenzene, toluene and n-hexane.

If you inhale enough of these, they can cause dizziness, nausea, headaches, or irritation of the eyes and throat. Long-term exposure, especially to benzene, can cause birth defects, neurological problems, blood disorders and even cancer.

Measurements of hazardous air pollutants near gas wells have generally found levels below the threshold the EPA considers dangerous. But there are tens of thousands of gas wells, and most aren't monitored for potential pollution.
